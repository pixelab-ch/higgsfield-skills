## The Science of the Hook

### The Completion Rate Curve

On TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts, the algorithm tracks when people stop
watching:

- **0–2 seconds:** The retention gate. If 50% bounce, the algorithm buries the video.
  If 90% stay, the algorithm promotes it.
- **2–5 seconds:** The "hook confirmation" phase — viewers decide if the video is worth
  their time.
- **5–15 seconds:** The engagement zone. Hooks land, curiosity peaks, emotional beats hit.
- **15+ seconds:** Retention falls sharply. Most videos should be 30 seconds or shorter
  for maximum algorithmic boost.

Videos with 80%+ completion rates get 2x algorithmic reach. Videos with 50% or lower
completion rates get shadow-banned. Your hook determines the entire trajectory.

### The Psychology of Attention

Five core triggers stop the scroll:

**1. Pattern Interrupt**
The brain is a prediction machine. When something violates the pattern, the brain says
"WAIT. That's anomalous. Pay attention." Examples: a glitch in reality, something moving
impossibly fast or slow, a color explosion in a gray world, a person doing something
impossible, sound cutting off suddenly.

**2. Curiosity Gap**
When the brain detects missing information, it wants to fill the gap. A hook creates
a question the viewer cannot answer until they keep watching. Examples: "Wait, why is
she crying?", "What's going to happen next?", showing 60% of a revelation forces the
brain to keep watching for the final 40%.

**3. Emotional Trigger**
Emotions trump logic. The amygdala (emotion center) processes stimuli faster than the
prefrontal cortex (logic center). Emotional hooks bypass the conscious scrolling decision:
cute overload (dopamine), fear or tension (adrenaline), awe or wonder (present-moment
focus), humor (relief, bonding), inspiration (aspiration). Emotional hooks are 3x more
likely to generate shares and comments.

**4. The Contrast Principle**
Humans perceive by comparison. A quiet sound followed by loud sound registers as LOUDER
than if the loud sound played alone. This hijacks attention: silence broken by a bang,
stillness broken by movement, dull color broken by a flash, whisper followed by a shout.
The contrast must happen in the first 2 seconds.

**5. Movement Detection**
Evolution wired us to notice movement. In a field of static images, movement grabs
attention in 200 milliseconds (faster than conscious thought). The first frame or first
transition must include movement: fast cuts, spinning motion, objects flying into frame,
character movement toward camera, on-screen text appearing with motion.

## The Hook Encyclopedia: 25+ Proven Patterns

Mix and match. Combine multiple patterns in one 2-second hook for maximum impact.

### Visual Shock Hooks

**1. Impossible Scale**
Make something appear impossibly large or impossibly small in the first frame.

Prompt cue: Open with an extreme wide shot showing a person standing next to a MASSIVE
object that should be tiny — 50x normal size. Camera pulls back slowly to reveal the
absurd scale. Use high contrast lighting. First 2 seconds: reveal the object, first
shocked reaction from person. Sound: deep bass hit as camera pulls back.

**2. Impossible Physics**
Show something doing what is physically impossible.

Prompt cue: Open with a person or object defying gravity. Water flowing upward. A person
walking on the ceiling. A car balancing on two wheels. Slow-motion capture the first
moment of impossibility. First 2 seconds: normal expectation, then cuts to impossible
action. Sound: notification "ding" as physics breaks.

**3. Color Explosion**
Grayscale world suddenly flooded with color.

Prompt cue: Start with desaturated gray color palette. At exactly 1.5 seconds, explosion
of color floods the frame — ONE bright color dominates (neon pink, electric blue, lime
green). Use hard cuts and speed ramps. First 2 seconds: building tension in grayscale,
then color blast. Sound: "whoosh" or electronic stab as color appears.

**4. Unexpected Reveal**
Camera positioned to hide what is actually happening until the last frame.

Prompt cue: Tight camera framing obscures what is actually occurring. Viewer sees hands
doing something, face reacting, but cannot piece together the full context. At 1.8
seconds, camera pulls back to reveal the full picture — completely different than
expected. First 2 seconds: confusion building, last 0.2 seconds: reveal shock. Sound:
dramatic stab or laugh track.

**5. Extreme Close-up Zoom**
Start impossibly close on texture, zoom out to reveal subject.

Prompt cue: Open with extreme macro shot — texture, pores, fabric weave, water droplets.
Completely unrecognizable what you are looking at. At 1 second, rapid zoom out reveals
the subject (person, object, landscape). First 2 seconds: mystery texture, rapid reveal.
Sound: ascending tone or metallic whoosh matching zoom speed.

**6. Split Screen Transformation**
Left side shows "before", right side shows "after", changes happen in real-time.

Prompt cue: Open on split screen. Left: unfinished, messy, or undesirable state. Right:
finished, polished, or desirable state. Begin transition at 0.5 seconds showing the
transformation happening live across the middle line. First 2 seconds: show the
before/after split, then transformation begins. Sound: satisfying transition sound or
music build.

**7. Freeze Frame Break**
Video appears frozen or paused, then suddenly unfreezes into fast motion.

Prompt cue: Open with video playing normally at 1x speed for 1.2 seconds. At 1.3
seconds, freeze frame holds for 0.3 seconds (appears to stutter or pause). At 1.7
seconds, unfrozen with dramatic speed ramp to 4x or 8x speed. First 2 seconds: normal
action, freeze, explosion of speed. Sound: scratching record sound at freeze, then
rapid-fire sound at speed ramp.

### Curiosity Gap Hooks

**8. Half-Reveal**
Show only 50–70% of something, force viewer to keep watching for the rest.

Prompt cue: Frame is positioned to show an incomplete action or object. Off-screen is
the crucial detail. Person's hand holds something we cannot quite see, face shows extreme
emotion but we cannot see what they are looking at, body does something but we cannot
see what is being done. First 2 seconds: intrigue builds from incompleteness. Sound:
ambient uncertainty or tension building.

**9. Wait For It Setup**
Create verbal or visual setup that demands continuation.

Prompt cue: Person or on-screen text makes a setup statement: "You won't believe what
happens next", "Wait until you see this", "I wasn't expecting that", "You have to see
the ending." The setup creates a question in the viewer's mind. First 2 seconds:
statement + wide-eyed reaction or dramatic pause. Sound: suspenseful music sting or
notification alert.

**10. Before/After Tease**
Show the "after", then cut to "before", forcing the audience to want to see the
transformation.

Prompt cue: Open with a shocking "after" image (person transformed, room redecorated,
makeup applied, object completed). Viewer's brain asks "how did that happen?" At 0.8
seconds, cut to "before" state, then begin showing the process. First 2 seconds:
shocking after, curiosity peak, transition to before. Sound: record scratch or flashback
transition sound.

**11. Ladder Reveal**
Each cut reveals a little more information, building mystery toward a punchline or climax.

Prompt cue:
- Cut 1 (0–0.5 s): Extreme close-up, unrecognizable
- Cut 2 (0.5–1.0 s): Slightly wider, slightly clearer but still mysterious
- Cut 3 (1.0–1.5 s): Even wider, identity becoming clear
- Cut 4 (1.5–2.0 s): Full reveal

Sound: ascending musical notes or progression toward climactic note.

**12. Misdirection Hook**
Set up one expectation, deliver something completely different.

Prompt cue: Establish a premise visually or verbally. Person talks about making
breakfast, cuts show cooking setup, then reveals they are actually making breakfast for
their 47 pet rabbits. Or: video appears to be about renovation, but it is actually about
rescuing kittens. First 2 seconds: establish false expectation. Sound: sound effects
matching the false expectation, then plot twist sound.

### Emotional Trigger Hooks

**13. Cute Overload**
Extreme cuteness in the first frame.

Prompt cue: Open on something undeniably cute: baby animal, tiny object, gentle
expression, innocent action. Use soft lighting, warm colors, and gentle motion. First 2
seconds: cuteness hits immediately. Sound: gentle warm music or "awww" sound, children's
laughter, or cute sound effect.

**14. Fear or Tension**
Activate the fight-or-flight response.

Prompt cue: Open with an unsettling visual, sudden sound, or dangerous setup. Person
about to do something risky, creepy sound building, camera panning to reveal a threat,
jump scare setup. Use high contrast shadows, dark colors, and tense music. First 2
seconds: danger is palpable. Sound: ominous music, creepy sound design, jump scare
sound, or tense silence.

**15. Awe/Wonder**
Something so beautiful or massive that it triggers awe.

Prompt cue: Open on something breathtaking — vast landscape, intricate detail, cosmic
scale, natural beauty, human achievement. Use cinematic framing, dramatic lighting, and
camera movement that conveys the scale or beauty. First 2 seconds: awe-struck moment.
Sound: orchestral swell, choir, or ambient wonder music.

**16. Humor/Absurdity**
Make people laugh in the first 2 seconds.

Prompt cue: Open with a funny premise, joke setup, absurd visual, or comedic character.
Timing is crucial. Use exaggeration, unexpected reactions, or physical comedy. First 2
seconds: setup or punchline lands. Sound: comedic sound effects, laugh track, or
comedic music.

**17. Inspiration/Aspiration**
Show someone achieving, becoming, or receiving something the viewer wants.

Prompt cue: Open on an aspirational moment — person achieving goal, transformation
complete, dream realized. Use empowering music, confident movement, and clear joy. First
2 seconds: moment of achievement. Sound: empowering music, victory sound, or
inspirational audio quote.

**18. Shock/Disgust**
Trigger a visceral reaction (use carefully).

Prompt cue: Open with something jarring, gross, or shocking (but not harmful). Messy
texture reveal, unexpected food combination, bizarre creature, unsettling visual. First
2 seconds: shock registers, viewer's brain cannot look away. Sound: gag reflex sound
effect, scream, retching sound, or discordant noise.

### Pattern Interrupt Hooks

**19. Reverse Motion**
Action plays backward at normal speed or fast.

Prompt cue: Film a recognizable action or sequence. Reverse it at 1x or 2x speed.
Person walking backward smoothly, water flowing upward, makeup being un-applied, object
being destroyed then reconstructed in reverse. First 2 seconds: brain recognizes action
is backward (pattern violation). Sound: reversed audio, backward speech, or forward
audio running over backward video creates cognitive dissonance.

**20. Glitch Effect**
Digital glitching in the visual or audio.

Prompt cue: Use digital glitch effects — pixel displacement, color separation, frame
duplication, digital distortion. Keep it brief (0.2–0.5 seconds) but impactful. Reality
glitches, revealing truth, person "buffers", video "corrupts". First 2 seconds: glitch
registers, brain says something is wrong. Sound: glitch sound design, digital artifacts,
data corruption sounds.

**21. Negative Space Break**
Composition changes dramatically.

Prompt cue: Start with subject centered or in expected position. Quickly cut to subject
in unexpected position (edge of frame, far background, diagonal), or negative space
dominates. First 2 seconds: composition expectation broken. Sound: spatial audio effect,
directional sound matching new composition.

**22. Audio Jump**
Sound is dramatically different from expectation.

Prompt cue: Visual suggests one audio (quiet, peaceful video = calm music expected),
but audio is completely different (intense bass drop, scream, explosion sound). First 2
seconds: audio/visual mismatch registers. Sound: bass hit, sound effect change, dialogue
cut-off, or genre-shift music.

**23. Rapid Cuts**
Quick cuts (0.2–0.3 seconds each) in first 2 seconds.

Prompt cue: First 2 seconds contain 4–8 quick cuts showing different moments, angles,
or contexts. Each cut is brief enough to keep visual fresh but hold narrative thread.
Person preparing (shot of clothes, shot of makeup, shot of final look), or ingredients
being assembled, or steps in a process. Sound: rhythmic music matching cut speed, or
rapid sound effects per cut.

**24. Focus Shift**
Depth of field changes rapidly.

Prompt cue: Open with camera focused on foreground (something in focus, background
blurred). Quickly shift focus to background object (background becomes sharp, foreground
blurs). Forces eye to move and reprocess what it is looking at. First 2 seconds: focus
shift surprises. Sound: camera focus sound, or audio matching object coming into focus.

### Direct Address Hooks

**25. Camera Look**
Character makes eye contact with camera, breaking the fourth wall.

Prompt cue: Start with character focused on activity, unaware of camera. At 1.2 seconds,
character turns and makes direct eye contact with camera. Hold for 0.8 seconds. First 2
seconds: moment of connection. Sound: pause in background sound, then resumption, or
intimate audio (whisper, conspiratorial tone).

**26. Direct Question**
Character asks viewer a question directly.

Prompt cue: At 0.5–1.0 seconds, character directly addresses camera/viewer: "Wanna know
why?", "Have you ever...?", "What would you do?", "Can you guess?" The question is
personalized and demands mental response. First 2 seconds: setup, then question lands.
Sound: conversational tone, pause for "answer", then continuation.

**27. On-Screen Text Command**
Bold text appears commanding viewer action.

Prompt cue: At 0.3 seconds, large bold text appears: "WAIT", "LOOK", "LISTEN", "WATCH
THIS", "PAY ATTENTION", "STOP SCROLLING". Text uses sans-serif bold font, high contrast
color (white/yellow on dark, black on bright). First 2 seconds: text command, then
payoff. Sound: text appearance sound, or sudden audio focus.

## Master Template: The Perfect 2-Second Hook

Use this structure as your baseline. Modify based on hook type.

```
[0.0–0.3 s] ATTENTION GRAB
- Visual shock, sound surprise, or pattern interrupt
- First frame/sound is unmissable
- One primary stimulus (not multiple competing elements)

[0.3–0.8 s] CURIOSITY BUILD
- Incomplete information revealed
- Question posed (visual or verbal)
- Emotional setup begins
- Expectation established (to be subverted or confirmed)

[0.8–1.5 s] MOMENTUM
- Confirm the hook is real (not clickbait)
- Move toward answer or climax
- Sound design reaches crescendo
- Visual energy sustained or increased

[1.5–2.0 s] COMMITMENT MOMENT
- Viewer has decided: keep watching or scroll
- Deliver on the hook's promise
- Leave viewer wanting MORE
- Create transition into full video narrative

TECHNICAL SPECS:
- Duration: exactly 2 seconds for the hook
- Aspect ratio: 9:16 (vertical, mobile-first) for most platforms
- Cuts: 3–5 total cuts in 2 seconds
- Sound: must have audio (silence is risky)
- On-screen text: optional, max 2 text elements
- Color: high contrast, intentional palette
- Movement: always present (not static)
- Pacing: accelerates toward 2-second mark
```

## Retention Techniques: Beyond the Hook

The hook gets viewers to watch past 2 seconds. These techniques keep them watching to
30+ seconds.

### The Curiosity Ladder

Once you have hooked with the first 2 seconds, maintain curiosity with sequential
revelations:
- 2–5 s: Confirm hook is real, escalate slightly
- 5–10 s: New question emerges (mini-cliff)
- 10–15 s: Partial answer, but new mystery
- 15–20 s: Major reveal or twist
- 20–30 s: Payoff and final satisfaction

### Pattern Breaks Throughout

Introduce pattern breaks at:
- 5-second mark (new visual/sound surprise)
- 10-second mark (tonal shift, mood change, new information)
- 15-second mark (climax or major reveal)

### Emotional Escalation Arc

- 0–2 s: Attention
- 2–5 s: Interest
- 5–10 s: Emotional investment (fear, joy, curiosity)
- 10–15 s: Emotional peak or twist
- 15–30 s: Resolution (satisfaction, answer, punchline, awe)

### Character Investment

If the video features a character:
- Show personality immediately
- Make them relatable (struggle, humor, authenticity)
- Let character drive narrative (not just voiceover about them)
- Show character's emotion/reaction to events

### Pacing and Cuts

- Average cut length: 0.5–1.0 seconds (fast enough for energy, slow enough to digest)
- Acceleration pattern: slow → medium → fast (matching music/energy)
- Do not cut on silence (cut on sound)
- Use transitions that match content (not random fancy transitions)

### Audio as Narrative

- Music should build throughout (not static)
- Sound effects should be satisfying or surprising
- Dialogue should be clear and engaging
- Silence is only powerful if followed by impactful sound

### Text as Retention

Strategic text keeps eyes on screen:
- 0.5–1.0 s: First text (hook reinforcement or question)
- 2.5–3.5 s: Second text (escalation or new question)
- 5.0–6.0 s: Third text (if pacing allows)
- Text should not just repeat audio — add new information
- Avoid on-screen text after 15 s (looks cluttered)

## Sound Strategy: Audio Hooks

Sound is 50% of the impact. A bad sound design ruins a great visual hook.

### Trending Audio Strategy

Why trending audio matters:
- TikTok algorithm boosts videos using trending audio
- Viewers recognize and enjoy familiar sounds
- Trending audio creates context/association
- But: using trending audio reduces originality (use when chasing algorithmic reach,
  not when building brand)

Using trending audio in hooks: the audio should start within the first 0.5 seconds.
Do not let the hook play silent then add audio.

### Original Audio Strategy

Why original audio matters:
- Builds personal brand (your signature sound)
- Stands out from competitors using trending audio
- Creates loyalty (viewers recognize your sound)
- More evergreen (does not feel dated when trend passes)

Original audio methods:
- Voiceover with personality (not robotic narration)
- Music producer/composer creates custom track
- Spoken dialogue from characters in video
- Real-world sounds (ASMR, ambient, foley)
- Combination: voice + licensed music underneath

### Silence-to-Impact

Strategic silence can be the most powerful audio tool:
- 0.0–1.2 s: Audio playing (music, sound effects, dialogue)
- 1.2–1.5 s: Complete silence (3 frames of quiet)
- 1.5–2.0 s: Sudden loud sound or impactful audio

The silence makes the sound that follows feel LOUDER and more shocking.

### ASMR Audio Hooks

ASMR sounds that trigger attention and relaxation:
- Cutting (knife through soft material, satisfying slices)
- Tapping (nails, pencil, fingertips on surface)
- Scratching (fabric, material, chalk)
- Whispering (intimate, quiet voice)
- Crinkling (paper, plastic, cellophane)
- Water (splashing, pouring, flowing)
- Footsteps (soft walking on different surfaces)

ASMR hook timing: audio should start immediately, first sound should be recognizable and
satisfying, volume should be intimate (not loud), continue throughout hook (not
intermittent).

### Dialogue Hooks

Spoken word can be the hook itself:
- Character speaks first line of hook (conversational, direct, personality-driven)
- Voiceover narrates hook (omniscient, guiding viewer)
- Character questions camera (engagement, involvement)
- Character exclaims/reacts (emotion-first)

Dialogue hook rules: First spoken words must land before 0.8 seconds. Dialogue should
be clear, not mumbled. Personality should come through. Avoid corporate tone. Authenticity
beats perfection.

## Text Overlay Strategy

On-screen text amplifies hooks. Used wrong, it distracts.

### Text Overlay Timing

**0.5–0.7 s: First Text**
- Large, bold, high contrast
- Sets hook expectation or poses question
- Examples: "WAIT FOR IT", "THIS WILL SHOCK YOU", "I WASN'T READY", "LITERALLY IMPOSSIBLE"
- Duration on screen: 0.5–1.5 seconds

**1.2–1.5 s: Second Text (optional)**
- Secondary hook or emotional escalation
- Examples: "THE ENDING THOUGH", "I CAN'T", "NO WAY", "THIS CHANGES EVERYTHING"
- Duration on screen: 0.5–1.0 seconds

Avoid text after 1.5 seconds in the hook phase (full video can have more text).

### Text Design for Virality

**Font:** Sans-serif, bold weight (Arial Bold, Helvetica Bold, Montserrat Bold). Avoid
thin fonts, script fonts, serif fonts (hard to read). Size: large enough to read on
phone — test on a 5-inch screen.

**Color:** High contrast — white text on dark background, black text on light background.
Add black outline/stroke to text to make it pop. Single color is better than gradient.

**Motion:** Text should appear with animation (pop, slide, scale). Appearance animation:
0.1–0.3 s. Hold: 0.3–1.0 s (readable time). Exit: optional (fade out works).

**Content:** All caps (more scannable, more impactful). 2–4 words max. Verb-forward
("WAIT FOR IT" beats "YOU WON'T BELIEVE THIS"). Emoji optional — use sparingly.

### Platform-Specific Text

**TikTok:** Text is expected — use it. Multiple texts create energy. CapCut's native text
tools work great.

**Instagram Reels:** Text can be subtle (less text than TikTok). Branded font if it is a
brand creator. Text should feel polished, not casual.

**YouTube Shorts:** Text can support but not dominate. Title text at top is okay. Avoid
too much text (looks spammy).

## Common Mistakes and Fixes

**Mistake 1: The False Promise Hook**
Hook promises something exciting, video does not deliver. Fix: Deliver on the hook
promise within 5 seconds. The hook is a contract; breaking it causes algorithm penalty
and viewer rage-quit.

**Mistake 2: Starting With Talking Head**
First 2 seconds is person talking to camera with no visual interest. Fix: Start with
visual shock or action. Talking heads lose 80% of viewers by 2 seconds. If you must
have voiceover, pair it with compelling visuals.

**Mistake 3: No Sound**
Hook is visually interesting but silent or has quiet ambient sound. Fix: Sound is 50%
of impact. Add ASMR, music, or sound effects. Silence is only powerful if followed by
sudden loud sound.

**Mistake 4: Bad Audio Quality**
Voiceover is muffled, background noise is loud, or sound is imbalanced. Fix: Invest in
good microphone. Audio quality signals professionalism (or lack thereof). Bad audio
equals faster bounce rate.

**Mistake 5: Shaky Camera**
First frame of hook shows unstable camera movement. Fix: Use tripod, gimbal, or
extremely steady hand. If you want movement, make it intentional and smooth.

**Mistake 6: Text Too Small or Low Contrast**
On-screen text is unreadable on mobile phone. Fix: Large font, bold weight, high
contrast. Test on actual phone. If you squint, text is too small.

**Mistake 7: Hook Takes Too Long**
Hook does not hook until 5+ seconds in. Fix: First 2 seconds is do-or-die. If your
hook takes 5 seconds to set up, lose the setup. Get straight to the interesting part.

**Mistake 8: Trending Audio Mismatch**
Using trending audio that does not match the hook's vibe. Fix: Audio must match
emotional tone. If using trending audio, ensure it aligns with your hook's
message/feeling.

**Mistake 9: Over-Edited Hook**
So many effects, transitions, and cuts that the actual hook is lost. Fix: Simplicity
beats complexity. The hook itself is interesting enough. Maximum 1–2 key effects.

**Mistake 10: Unclear Premise**
After watching 2 seconds, viewer has no idea what the video is about. Fix: Context must
be clear or curiosity must be burning. "What is this about?" should be answerable or
urgently interesting by 2 seconds.
