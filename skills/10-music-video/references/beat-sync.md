## Beat-Visual Synchronization Philosophy

At its core, a music video is a **fusion of audio and visual rhythm**. They are not
separate — they are one art form.

- **Every beat is an opportunity for visual change.** Even subtle: a blink, a light
  flicker, a slight rotation, a color shift.
- **Silence is as important as sound.** Use darkness, stillness, and minimal movement in
  quiet moments.
- **Syncopation creates visual interest.** If the music plays against the beat, your
  visuals can too.
- **The drop is sacred.** Every listener expects impact at the drop. Your visuals must
  deliver.
- **Energy should mirror song structure.** Verse = lower energy; chorus = peak energy;
  bridge = shift; final chorus = highest energy; outro = resolution or impact-end.

## 2-Second Hook Framework for Music Videos

Every great music video has moments of maximum impact — visual hooks that grab attention
and sync perfectly to the music. These are typically 2-second windows of highest intensity.

### 12 Essential Beat-Synced Hooks

**1. Beat Drop Visual Explosion**
The moment a bass drop hits after silence or build. Particles explode outward, color
saturates, light flashes, geometric shapes burst into frame.
`"Beat drop at 0:32: massive particle explosion outward in gold and red, geometric shapes shatter across the screen."`

**2. Silent Intro Then Bass Hit**
Minimal production, then sudden low-end impact. Dark scene suddenly illuminated, camera
shakes, color floods in.
`"First 3 seconds silent and dark blue, then at 0:03 the bass hits and white light floods the frame."`

**3. Rapid Flash Cuts on Hi-Hats**
Fast percussion demands quick visual cuts. Jump cuts between positions, rhythm-matched
flashes, strobe effects.
`"During hi-hat section (0:15-0:25): rapid jump cuts between close-up performer, wide shot, and colored light flashes, synced to each hat hit."`

**4. Slow-Mo to Real-Time on Beat**
Slow motion builds tension, then snaps to real-time on impact.
`"Verse in slow motion (50% speed), then on the kick at 0:45 it snaps to real-time with a sharp cut upward."`

**5. Visual Glitch on Synth Stab**
Sharp electronic sound matched to visual glitch. Screen tearing, color shift, frame
duplication, distortion.
`"On each synth stab in the pre-chorus, the image glitches with horizontal tearing and a purple-to-cyan color shift."`

**6. Dramatic Performer Reveal on Downbeat**
Empty stage/scene, then sudden cut to performer mid-movement at the downbeat.
`"Empty red warehouse, then on the main downbeat at 0:20, cut to close-up of performer's face in harsh spotlight."`

**7. Orbital Camera Movement on Chorus**
Camera orbits subject in time with the beat. Creates centrifugal feel.
`"Chorus: camera orbits performer clockwise, completing one full rotation every 4 bars, with speed increasing on final bar."`

**8. Color Shift on Harmonic Change**
Different chord or key triggers color change. Gradual or instant palette shift.
`"When harmony modulates up a half-step at 0:50, all colors shift from cool blues to warm oranges and golds."`

**9. Geometry Morphing to Melody**
Geometric shapes move, grow, rotate to track the melody contour.
`"Abstract geometric shape grows taller as the melody rises, shrinks during the fall, synced precisely to the vocal line."`

**10. Depth Zoom on Energy Peak**
Fast zoom into frame creates tunnel vision effect at highest energy moment.
`"At the final drop (1:50), zoom rapidly into a tunnel of neon light, moving at tempo so the forward motion feels like acceleration."`

**11. Inverted/Negative Flash on Peak Moment**
Screen inverts for 0.5 seconds at climactic beat, then snaps back.
`"On the final kick of the breakdown at 1:45, the entire image inverts — white becomes black — then snaps back."`

**12. Cascading Reveals on Stacked Vocals**
Multiple vocal layers reveal visual elements in sequence.
`"As each vocal harmony enters during the chorus, a new light source reveals itself: main vocal = white, second = blue, third = gold."`

## Beat-Sync Techniques (Describing Visual Beats in Prompts)

### Technique 1: Beat Reference Timing

Direct timing references anchor visuals to the audio:
```
"At 0:32 (the drop), [visual action] syncs with the kick drum"
"From 1:15-1:30 (the breakdown), particles respond to the bass"
"At 0:45 when the snare cracks, the screen flashes white"
```

Bar-based references (if time signature is known):
```
"Every downbeat (every 4 beats), rotate 360 degrees"
"On beats 2 and 4 (snare hits), color shifts occur"
```

### Technique 2: Beat Density Matching

Match visual density to beat density:
```
"During hi-hat section, create visual cuts or flashes every half-beat"
"During chorus, kick drum is every beat: make one visual change per kick"
"Bridge is sparse (2-bar phrasing), so use slower, larger camera movements"
```

### Technique 3: Energy Mapping

Describe how visual intensity matches musical intensity:
```
"Build visual intensity as drums layer in: minimal particles (0:00),
add bass response (0:10), rapid cuts (0:20), explosion (0:32)"
"Energy dips at 1:00 (quiet bridge): slow cuts, desaturate, reduce particles"
"At 1:45 (final drop), maximum visual intensity: fastest cuts, brightest colors"
```

### Technique 4: Frequency-Responsive Effects

Describe frequency ranges that control visual effects:
```
"Particles respond to bass (low frequencies): expand outward when bass hits"
"Melody controls height of geometric shapes: rise and fall with vocal line"
"Drums control color cycling: each kick shifts to next color in palette"
```

### Technique 5: Syncopation Visuals

Match visual accents to syncopated (off-beat) moments:
```
"Snare is syncopated (off-beat), so create visual accents on the off-beat:
camera shakes or color flashes that surprise the viewer"
```

### Technique 6: Break and Build Tracking

Describe how visuals evolve through song sections:
```
"Verse (0:00-0:20): minimal visuals, color shifts only
Build/Pre-Chorus (0:20-0:30): add movement, increase cut rate
Chorus (0:30-0:50): maximum visual density
Verse 2 (0:50-1:10): pull back to mid-level (not full reset)
Final Chorus (1:10-end): peak energy, all visuals combined"
```

### Technique 7: Sub-Bass Depth

For heavy low-end tracks, describe visual depth changes:
```
"When sub-bass hits (0:32), zoom camera forward into scene at tempo"
"Bass sustains 0:45-1:05, maintain forward zoom momentum throughout"
"Each bass note is a separate zoom push: 7 pushes in 7 seconds"
```

## Energy Arcs Matching Song Structure

| Song section | Visual energy | Cut rate | Color/lighting |
|-------------|--------------|----------|----------------|
| Verse | Lower energy, intimate | Slow (one cut per phrase) | Softer, cooler |
| Pre-chorus / Build | Rising, building | Increasing | Shifting toward peak palette |
| Chorus | Peak energy, maximum impact | Fast (per beat) | Full saturation, peak intensity |
| Bridge | Change of pace, shift | Sparse or sudden change | New palette or desaturated |
| Final chorus | Highest energy, widest dynamic | Fastest cuts | Maximum brightness / saturation |
| Outro | Resolution or impact-end | Fading or sharp end | Fade to calm or explosive cut |

## Performance vs Narrative vs Abstract

Choose visual strategy based on song type:

### Performance Videos
**Focus:** Artist(s) performing the music. Best for live energy, charisma, dance,
instrumental virtuosity.

Key camera techniques:
- Push-in during emotional moments; slow orbit for power moments
- Quick cuts (wide → extreme close-up) during energy peaks
- Lock-off shots during verses; dynamic movement during chorus
- Lighting: spotlight effect isolating performer; rim lighting for dimension

Prompt cue: `"performance-focused, showcase the performer, concert lighting, live energy"`

### Narrative Videos
**Focus:** Story that unfolds alongside the music. Best for emotional songs, cinematic
concepts, storytelling tracks.

Story arc structures:
- **Cinematic Arc:** Setup → Escalation → Peak → Resolution (across song duration)
- **Metaphor/Symbolic:** Surreal setup → intensification → symbolic meaning → resolution
- **Character Transformation:** Before → Catalyst → Transformation → After
- **Dual Narrative:** Two characters/timelines that converge or contrast at key moments
- **Time Loop:** Repeating scene with subtle variations revealing deeper understanding

Prompt cue: `"cinematic narrative, story-driven, character journey, visual metaphor"`

### Abstract / Visualizer
**Focus:** Pure visual expression — particles, geometry, color, frequency-responsive effects.
No narrative or performer. Best for instrumental, experimental, or audio-focused tracks.

Core techniques:
- Particles responding to bass (radial expansion on kick)
- Geometry morphing to melody contour
- Color cycling with harmony changes
- Fractal/recursive patterns zooming on build, exploding on drop
- Laser/light-beam effects rotating at tempo
- Fluid dynamics/smoke simulating audio shape

Prompt cue: `"abstract, generative, particles responding to bass, visualizer, color-responsive"`

## Camera Techniques for Music Videos

| Technique | Use when |
|-----------|----------|
| Rhythm-Matched Push-In | Verse (slow push over 8 bars) → Chorus (same distance in 4 bars, more urgency) |
| Orbital Motion on Chorus | Camera orbits subject matching tempo; speeds up as energy builds |
| Whip Pan on Transition | Verse-to-chorus or location change; camera whips 0.2 s then reveals new shot |
| Push-In on Vocal Peak | Camera arrives at closest framing exactly on the emotional peak |
| Zoom Parallax / Depth Zoom | Slow forward zoom during build → explosive zoom acceleration on drop |
| Lock-Off (Stationary) | Introspective or intimate moments; stillness forces attention to expression |
| Handheld / Intentional Shake | Chaotic or intense moments; micro-movements scale with emotional intensity |
| Synchronized Saccades | Camera jerks on snare hits / shakes on kicks; most effective in driving sections |

## Multi-Segment Strategy for Full Songs

veo3_1 generates clips of 4, 6, or 8 seconds. A full music video requires multiple
segments:

1. **Map the song structure:** Identify intro, verses, pre-choruses, choruses, bridge,
   outro — note each section's start time and approximate length.
2. **Assign clips per section:** A 30-second chorus = 3–4 clips of 6–8 s each.
3. **Maintain visual continuity:** Use consistent color palette, lighting style, and
   character description across clips in the same section.
4. **Vary within sections:** Each clip should add a new camera angle, beat hook, or
   energy escalation — avoid generating the same prompt twice.
5. **Credit planning:** Each clip is a separate generation. Budget accordingly before
   starting a full song.
